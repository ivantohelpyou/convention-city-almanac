# CommonScore Article Notes

## The Headline Number

Seattle scores **27**. With the Arch converted to a commons: **50**. One building nearly doubles the score. No other city in the dataset has that kind of latent capacity sitting idle.

## The Setup

32 cities scored across 16 dimensions. The question: *if I live here and want to DO something this week — not watch, not consume, but participate — is there space for me?*

No city has broken 50. Singapore leads at 44 through comprehensive government engineering (hawker centres, community clubs, SkillsFuture). Helsinki scores 30 — and it has Oodi, a purpose-built commons. Seattle scores 27 without one.

**Helsinki > Seattle because Oodi.** That's the gap. One building.

|---|---|---|---|

| 2-4 | London, Mexico City, New York | 38 | Institutional depth — libraries, universities, markets accumulated over decades |
| 14 | Helsinki | 30 | Oodi proves one building can move a city 12 points |

**Austin = Seattle** is the competitive hook. Same score, different strengths. Austin has food trucks (1,000+) and Barton Springs. Seattle has Pike Place and a stronger library system. Austin is building a $1.6B convention center. Seattle has one it could repurpose.

## The Arch Scenario

What the Arch has: 205,000 sq ft, high ceilings, loading docks, industrial kitchens, acoustically isolated rooms, Walk Score 98, three blocks from Horizon House.

What happens to the score:

| Dimension | Now | With Arch as Commons | What changes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Food | 3.5 | 7.7 | Aramark kitchens → independent operator food hall |
| Civic | 3.5 | 7.7 | $1,000/day rooms → community rate |
| Industry Networking | 3.4 | 2.5 | Summit keeps hosting conventions |
| Makers | 1.0 | 3.2 | Loading docks → maker labs at scale |
| Robotics | 0.4 | 2.4 | MassRobotics-style lab in high-bay space |
| **Total** | **27** | **~50** | |

The convention center contributes 16% of Seattle's score today (Industry + Mega only). The other 84% was built by libraries, community centers, markets, makerspaces, music venues, and parks. What if the building served the other 14 dimensions too?

## The Macy's Fallback

If the Arch isn't available: Macy's at 3rd & Pine. 300,000+ sq ft, downtown core, blocks from the Arch. Same framework applies. "You don't want to give us the Arch? Fine."

The CommonScore doesn't prescribe which building. It identifies which dimensions have the largest gap between proven demand and available space. The gap analysis works whether the candidate is a convention center, an empty department store, or a decommissioned power plant.

## Key Findings to Weave In

1. **Civic infrastructure is the strongest predictor.** Libraries with meeting rooms are the closest existing model to a commons. In 28 of 32 cities, Civic is top-2.

2. **Convention center size doesn't predict score.** McCormick Place (2.6M sq ft) scores 26. Oodi (185K sq ft) helps Helsinki score 30.

3. **The virtuous cycle.** Participation infrastructure makes a city more interesting to visitors who become repeat visitors who become residents. Convention-as-consumption is a dead end; convention-as-participation is a flywheel.

4. **"Music City" doesn't mean participation.** Nashville (14) and Austin (27) both brand around music. But open mics and rehearsal studios are different from watching bands at bars.

5. **The demand signals are already in Seattle.** Phoenix Comics (gaming, nightly, full), Capitol Hill Tool Library (makers, 2007, pay-what-you-can), AI House + AI Tinkerers (innovation, capacity-constrained, borrowed space). The bottleneck is never demand. It's always space.

4. **Here's what Helsinki did** (built Oodi, jumped 12 points, €98M)
5. **Here's what Seattle could do** (convert the Arch, jump from 27 to 50)
6. **Here's the fallback** (Macy's at 3rd & Pine — same framework, different building)

## Voice Notes

- The CommonScore is a compass, not a directory. Don't get lost in facility hours.
- The "I" in the question is a resident, not a tourist. A tourist eats at a hawker centre; a resident operates a stall.
- "Beat Boston" is a motivator. Boston is at 32. Seattle is at 27. The gap is 5 points — specific investments could close it.
- "Beat Austin" is the competitive frame. Same score, same tech-city profile, they're building a $1.6B convention center while we have one we could repurpose.
- The 30→50 number is the knockout. Save it for the turn.

## What This Is NOT

- Not an argument to demolish the convention center (DC did that — different story)
- Not an argument against conventions (Industry Networking is a real dimension, honestly scored)
- Not a tourism metric (participation is for residents)
- Not a directory of facilities (it's a compass)
